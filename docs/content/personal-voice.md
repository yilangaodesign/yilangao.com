# Section 17 - Personal Voice & Techniques

> Universal voice calibration for the portfolio. Applies to ALL case studies.
> This file is read during `case-study-authoring` Phase 3 and during
> `content-iteration` when the user provides raw draft text.

## 17.1 Voice Samples (calibration targets)

These are raw paragraphs of the user's writing, captured during the Elan
Design System case study rebuild (2026-04-04). They are the universal
register the system must aim for. Not identical style across all zones - the
body is always more restrained than the blurb - but the same personality.

If the output sounds like it could have been written by anyone, it hasn't
matched the calibration target.

**Sample 1 (blurb hook):**

> "Teaching Einstein how to build a design system...when he's 6 years old.
> Sounds ridiculous? That's what everyone is facing right now trying to spin
> up vibe coded apps. AI powerful? Yes. AI dumb? Also yes. AI is a genius
> with outstanding knowledge of the world... only if you taught it how to use
> its knowledge correctly."

What makes it work: Opens with a metaphor that creates an instant picture.
Two rhetorical questions in rapid succession. Punch-counterpunch ("Yes...
Also yes"). The authority comes from lived experience framing, not credential
listing.

**Sample 2 (pain + positioning):**

> "I have been vibe coding rigorously since GPT came out. A pain point I have
> is xxxx, horrible consistency... There're lots of skill out there, but...
> Afraid of the Tailwind blue violet color? I'm too. That's why I made this
> system. To save myself from the abyss of AI slop."

What makes it work: First-person narrative as authority signal. Cultural
in-group reference (Tailwind blue-violet) that filters the audience. Survival
framing ("save myself"). Sentence fragments as the dominant rhythm - each
fragment lands a beat.

**Sample 3 (blurb headline - protagonist framing):**

> "I saved the page my own team gave up on"

What makes it work: First-person verb creates a protagonist with stakes. "My
own team gave up on" supplies the resistance that makes "saved" feel earned,
not self-congratulatory. The emphasis lands on the situation (abandoned page),
not the achievement (saving it). 10 words, upper limit of blurb headline
range. Source: Lacework case study (2026-04-04).

**Sample 4 (section title - object substitution):**

> "Five Matryoshkas Deep"

What makes it work: "Matryoshka" replaces "nested navigation layers" with a
single concrete object the reader can see. The number (five) adds precision.
"Deep" frames the problem as burial, not just complexity. 3 words. Source:
Lacework case study (2026-04-04).

**Sample 5 (section title - verdict + staccato):**

> "Donut. Avoid at All Cost."

What makes it work: "Donut" as a one-word sentence heading. Deliberately
ambiguous (food or chart?). "Avoid at All Cost" is a verdict, not a
description. The body opener "Not the food. The chart." resolves the
ambiguity in 5 words. Shows the designer has opinions and states them as
rules. 5 words. Source: Lacework case study (2026-04-04).

## 17.2 Named Technique Library

These are generation tools. The AI should reach for them when writing first
drafts across ALL case studies.

### Body Rhythm Techniques (1-5)

Techniques for creating voice and personality within flowing prose. Applied
DURING the writing step (Phase 3).

### Technique 1: Punch-Counterpunch

Question-answer in under 10 words. "AI powerful? Yes. AI dumb? Also yes."

- **Use:** Hook openings, introducing a paradox, flipping an assumption.
- **Cap:** Max 2 in a row. After 2, shift to a longer sentence.
- **Applies to:** Zone 1 (blurb). Rarely in Zone 2 (scope). Never in Zone 3
  (section body).

### Technique 2: In-Group Signal

A cultural reference that makes the target reader think "that's me."
"Afraid of the Tailwind blue-violet?" / "every session starts from zero."

- **Use:** One per blurb. One per case study in section bodies (as a
  contrarian line opener).
- **The reference must be specific enough** that outsiders still understand
  the energy, even if they don't get the exact reference.
- **Applies to:** All zones. But in Zone 3, it replaces a contrarian line,
  not added on top of one.

### Technique 3: Survival Frame

"That's why I made this system. To save myself from the abyss."

- **Use:** Positioning a project as something the designer needed to exist,
  not something built for the portfolio. One per case study max. Always in
  the blurb body or scope statement, never in section bodies.
- **Compatible with luxury positioning:** the survival frame states motivation
  as fact ("I built this because X"), not as credential performance ("I
  heroically overcame Y").

### Technique 4: Escalation Beat

Three-beat temporal compression. "First time... wrote it down. Third time...
made it a rule. Now it checks before it touches a single color."

- **Use:** Showing growth, iteration, or compounding. Universally applicable
  across all project types.
- **Applies to:** All zones. Especially strong in Zone 3 section bodies
  because it demonstrates progression without editorializing.

### Technique 5: Wry Redirect

Rhetorical question that acknowledges absurdity or tension before the reader
can. "Sounds ridiculous?"

- **Use:** Once per blurb max. Zero in section bodies. The redirect must be
  followed by a sentence that answers the question or escalates the tension.

### Headline Composition Techniques (6-10 + modifiers)

Techniques for compressing entire arguments into single phrases. Applied
BEFORE writing (Phase 2 planning) because titles frame the argument each
section delivers. See `case-study.md` section 3.4 for the three title
archetypes (Object, Inversion, Verdict) and the Preview Title anti-pattern.

### Technique 6: Object Substitution (Synecdoche)

Replace a process or abstraction with the single concrete object that
contains the argument. The object BECOMES the headline.

"Five Matryoshkas Deep" instead of "Five Layers to Three."

- **Use:** When the section's argument centers on a vivid physical object or
  metaphor. The object must be immediately picturable - if the reader can't
  see it in one word, pick a different object.
- **Applies to:** Section titles (2-5 words). Rarely blurb headlines (too
  compressed for the 6-10 word range).
- **Not the same as** the "Physical, spatial language" vocabulary in
  `voice-style.md` section 13.1. That list is about word choice (use "layer"
  not "architecture"). Object Substitution is a compositional strategy - the
  object replaces the abstract argument entirely, at the headline level.

### Technique 7: Framework Inversion

Reference a well-known framework or conventional wisdom and flip it. The
headline is a thesis statement, not a question.

"Build Instead of Buy" inverts the standard "build vs. buy" decision
framework.

- **Use:** When the section's argument inverts a known framework or
  conventional wisdom. The framework must be recognizable to the target
  audience in under a second - if it needs explanation, it's not a framework
  inversion, it's jargon.
- **Applies to:** Section titles (2-5 words). Can stretch to blurb headlines
  if the inversion is sharp enough to carry 6-10 words.
- **The inversion must stake a position.** "Build vs. Buy" is a question.
  "Build Instead of Buy" is a verdict. The headline has already made the
  argument in 4 words.

### Technique 8: Verdict-as-Headline

The headline IS the opinion, not a preview of the opinion. State a design
judgment as if it were a law.

"Donut. Avoid at All Cost."

- **Use:** When the section arrives at a strong conclusion about a design
  decision, method, or approach. One verdict-style title per case study is
  strong. Two is fine. Three risks the case study reading as a listicle of
  hot takes.
- **Applies to:** Section titles (2-5 words) and blurb headlines (6-10
  words, when combined with a resolver clause or resistance framing).
- **Luxury guardrail:** Verdicts apply to DESIGN DECISIONS and METHODS,
  never to OUTCOMES or ACHIEVEMENTS. "Donut. Avoid at All Cost." = verdict
  about a chart type (design decision - passes luxury). "A Breakthrough in
  Dashboard Design" = verdict about own achievement (outcome editorializing -
  fails luxury). The line: you can judge your tools and methods; you cannot
  judge your own impact. See `case-study.md` section 3.8.

### Technique 9: Deliberate Ambiguity (modifier)

Momentary confusion through dual meaning, resolved within one sentence.
Applied on top of Techniques 6-8, not standalone.

"Donut. Avoid at All Cost." then "Not the food. The chart."

- **Use:** When a key word in the title can mean two things productively. The
  ambiguity must create a vivid picture (even the wrong one), and the
  resolver must arrive within one sentence (under 10 words). If resolution
  takes a full paragraph, it is scene-setting, not a pattern interrupt.
- **Applies to:** Both section titles and blurb headlines (as a modifier on
  top of the primary technique).
- **Cap:** One per case study. Two deliberate ambiguities in one case study
  makes the writer seem like they're doing a bit.
- **"Get to the point" compatibility:** The existing rule in `voice-style.md`
  says "No bland scene-setting." The escape clause applies: "A vivid
  metaphor that creates an instant picture is a hook, not scene-setting."
  Deliberate Ambiguity creates an instant picture (a donut), then
  recontextualizes it. The key constraint is immediate resolution.
- **Not the same as** Wry Redirect (Technique 5). Wry Redirect ACKNOWLEDGES
  absurdity before the reader can ("Sounds ridiculous?") - it is reactive,
  deflecting skepticism. Deliberate Ambiguity creates MISDIRECTION through
  dual meaning ("Donut" = food? chart?) - it is generative, creating
  curiosity. Different mechanisms.

### Technique 10: Protagonist Framing

"I [verb]ed" as dramatic casting with personal stakes against resistance.
The writer becomes a character in a story with an uncertain outcome.

"I saved the page my own team gave up on."

- **Use:** When the blurb needs to establish personal ownership and stakes.
  The verb must describe action-against-resistance, not achievement. The
  second half of the headline must supply the resistance or tension.
- **Applies to:** Blurb headlines only (6-10 words). Too long for section
  titles. Too first-person for the restrained register of Zone 3.
- **Luxury guardrail:** Remove "I [verb]ed" from the headline. Does the
  remainder describe a difficult situation? If yes, it passes luxury. If the
  remainder describes an accomplishment or just names a deliverable, it is
  credential listing and fails luxury.
  - "the page my own team gave up on" = difficult situation (PASS)
  - "the billing dashboard" = just a thing (FAIL)
  - "a migration nobody thought would work" = difficulty (PASS)
  - "the most beautiful navigation ever" = self-congratulation (FAIL)
- See `case-study.md` section 3.8 for the full Luxury Positioning Principle.

### Modifier: Staccato Authority

Fragment-as-sentence at the headline level. A single word or short phrase
punctuated as a complete sentence to signal conviction. Applied on top of
Techniques 6-8, not standalone.

"Donut." as a one-word sentence heading.

- **Use:** When the title can compress to 1-3 fragment-sentences with
  periods. The periods create authority - they say "this is a complete
  thought, no elaboration needed." Most effective combined with Verdict-as-
  Headline (Technique 8) or Object Substitution (Technique 6).
- **Applies to:** Both section titles and blurb headlines.
- **Not the same as** the fragment density rules in `voice-style.md` section
  13.1. Those rules govern BODY TEXT rhythm (2-3 fragments per section in
  Zone 3). Staccato Authority governs HEADLINE-LEVEL fragments where a
  single word becomes a complete sentence-heading. Different scale: body
  fragments are seasoning; headline fragments are structural.

## 17.4 Headline vs. Body Composition

Body techniques (1-5) and headline techniques (6-10) operate at different
compositional levels and at different points in the workflow.

**Body techniques** create rhythm and personality within flowing prose. They
are applied DURING writing (Phase 3). A paragraph with Punch-Counterpunch
or an Escalation Beat has personality; without them, it reads like a report.

**Headline techniques** compress entire arguments into single phrases. They
are applied BEFORE writing (Phase 2 planning) because titles frame the
argument each section needs to deliver. A section titled "Build Instead of
Buy" demands different body text than one titled "Why Not Just Keep Tableau."
The title sets the energy and the angle.

The default AI output for headlines is a descriptive summary ("Five Layers
to Three," "Reading the Speedometer"). These are Preview Titles - accurate
but not magnetic. The headline techniques exist to override this default.
If a first draft title describes the section content, it is a Preview Title
and must be rewritten using one of Techniques 6-10.

For the selection heuristic (which technique to use for which section), see
the case-study-authoring skill Phase 2 Step 4.

## 17.3 Refinement Protocol (two-tier constraint model)

When the user provides raw draft text as a modification to existing content,
the processing is different from a standard content fix. The user's raw text
carries voice energy that the refinement must preserve.

### Tier 1 - Enforce always (non-negotiable)

- Banned words (`voice-style.md` 13.1 table)
- Em dash ban (use regular dash with spaces)
- Thesis coherence (every sentence serves the thesis)
- Word count limits (blurb: 80 words, section body: 1-3 sentences)
- No credential performance / no editorializing outcomes (CAP-019)
- No buzzword misapplication (CAP-020)

### Tier 2 - Yield to user voice (preserve if the user wrote it that way)

- Fragment density (if the user's draft uses fragments for rhythm, keep the
  rhythm)
- Scene-setting (if the user's "scene" creates a vivid image, it's a hook)
- Rhetorical questions (the user uses them for engagement; preserve placement
  and energy)
- Cultural in-group references (preserve specific references even if they
  have a shelf life)
- Informal grammar that carries personality ("Me too." not "I agree." /
  contractions, spoken rhythm)
- Sentence rhythm (if the user's draft alternates short-short-long, preserve
  that pattern)

### The instruction

Fix grammar errors (typos, unclear antecedents, subject-verb disagreement).
Tighten logic connections where the draft drifts from the thesis. Enforce
Tier 1 constraints. But never restructure rhythm, substitute clinical
phrasing for vivid phrasing, or remove a cultural reference because it
doesn't match the formal rules. If a Tier 2 rule conflicts with the user's
draft, the user's draft wins.

### Voice-flattening test (run after every refinement)

Read the original draft and the refined version side by side. If the refined
version is less vivid, less specific, or less personal than the original, the
refinement over-corrected. Restore the original's energy and try again with
lighter editing. See also CAP-023 in `docs/content-anti-patterns.md`.
