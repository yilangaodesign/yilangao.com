# Appendix A: Reference Portfolios

### A.1 Visual Craft References

These portfolios demonstrate strong visual craft and portfolio-as-artifact principles. They are references for visual execution, not for visual-text ratio (which has shifted to ~60/40 for case studies).

#### Joseph Zhang — joseph.cv

**Why it works:**
- Company names do heavy lifting (Notion, Apple, Cursor, Azuki) — instant credibility
- Scope statements claim ownership and ambition simultaneously
- Named collaborators add legitimacy
- External validation via press links (Hypebeast, Highsnobiety, Yahoo Finance)
- Each project tells a different capability story
- The website itself is a custom-built artifact (not a template)

**Key language patterns:**
- "I led design at Skiff" — direct ownership claim
- "As the first full-time design hire" — seniority signal
- "I helped scale Skiff from beta to over +1,000,000 users" — impact metric
- "20 interactive graphics scattered across the marketing site, each individually coded in React" — specificity in captions

#### Ryo Lu — work.ryo.lu

**Why it works:**
- Portfolio IS the product (built inside Notion) — meta-demonstration of competence
- Extreme confidence in letting work speak visually
- Project entries organized by feature and year within each company
- Personal site (ryo.lu) shows personality AND technical capability

**Key insight from Dive Club interview:**
> "What I like seeing is work. Real work, pictures, ideally something I can play with, that's live. Not long writing case studies of standard product development process. I care a lot about all the details, how you present things, how you build your website. All the typography."

### A.2 Writing-Craft References

These references demonstrate the practitioner-writing tradition this brand belongs to. They are touchstones for prose quality, pacing, and the relationship between text and visuals.

- **Julia Evans (zines and blog)** — Dense, scannable, image-supported technical writing where every paragraph carries information and humor lowers the register without compromising substance. The closest single reference for case study writing rhythm.
- **Stripe engineering blog** — Contemporary digital expression of practitioner writing about hard technical problems, scoped to a busy reader's attention and supported by precise diagrams.
- **Figma blog** — Designer-engineer team writing about their own work in a way that respects the reader's time without sacrificing depth.
- **Linear Readme page** — The single best contemporary reference for pacing. ~1,500 words, one-to-three-sentence paragraphs, white space as beats. Proof that length and scannability are not enemies.
- **Maciej Ceglowski (Pinboard blog)** — Practitioner voice that is very funny, very precise, and unwilling to pretend the problems are simpler than they are. Post structure closely matches the argument-driven section headers approach.
- **Bret Victor (shorter essays)** — The form of an explanation is part of the explanation. Writing about technical work can itself be a craft act.

### A.3 Common Patterns Across References

1. Visual-text parity: case studies target ~60% visual, ~40% text. Text and images are near-equal partners.
2. Text is surgical, not explanatory — every word earns its place
3. No process documentation — no double diamond, no persona cards
4. Outcome-first framing — intro tells you what matters immediately
5. Section-based structure — not linear narrative, but discrete features/moments
6. Named collaborators — shows team context without being generic
7. External validation — live product links, press coverage, acquisition news
8. The portfolio itself is a design artifact — custom domains, careful typography, interactive elements
9. Practitioner authority — the writer built the thing they are writing about
